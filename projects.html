BLRS SIGBots - Change Up

Change Up was the 2020-2021 VEX Robotics season, and my first year on the Purdue SIGBots team. Although many competitions were canceled because of Covid, we were still able to compete in two tournaments this season. Our first tournament was hosted by Auburn University which we ended up winning. Our second competition was in place of the World Championship and was hosted at VEX headquarters in Greenville Texas. We ended up as finalists, losing in the final match but also won Skills Champion and the Think Award.

The objectives of the game this year were to pick up balls from the ground and score them in the nine goals located around the field as well as descore the opposing team's color from these goals. Bonus points are awarded to teams who can create rows, columns, or diagonals with their color. This game was much simpler than most games so the real challenge was scoring as efficiently as possible. One way this differed from my previous experience is that with the VEXU competition, teams are allowed to manufacture custom parts. This allowed us to use laser-cut metals and 3D-printed parts on our robots.

7701T Tesseract - Tower Takeover

Tower Takeover was the 2019-2020 VEX Robotics season. My team again qualified for both the Indiana State Championship and the VEX World Championship. We ended the season 29th in the world for individual skills out of around 10,000 international teams. Unfortunately, the 2020 VEX World Championship was canceled due to Covid-19 so we were unable to compete. This season we earned a total of 11 awards: Tournament Champion (x5), Tournament Finalist, Excellence Award (x2), Robot Skills Champion (x2), and the Judges Award. This was by far my most successful season, winning almost half of the competitions we went to.

The objectives of the game this year were to pick up cubes and stack them in specified areas as well as score the cubes in towers located around the field. We were able to create a robot that completed all of these tasks efficiently, scoring more points much faster than most if not all of the other teams.

7701T Tesseract - Turning Point

Turning Point was the 2018-2019 VEX Robotics season. My team, 7701T Tesseract, qualified for both the Indiana State Championship and the VEX World Championship. We placed 18th in the world for individual skills out of around 10,000 international teams. At the 2019 World Championship, we made it to the elimination matches where we finished as division semi-finalists. We earned a total of 3 awards this year: Excellence Award, Judges Award, and the Amaze Award.

The objectives of the game this year were to pick up and shoot balls at plastic flags to toggle them to your color, to pick up, flip, and place caps onto raised poles, and climb a platform at the end of the match. We used the engineering process to create the robot to effectively and efficiently complete all of these tasks.

Custom Mechanical Keyboard

This is a 3D printed, hand-wired, custom mechanical keyboard. I have always been interested in mechanical keyboards ever since I got my first desktop PC. The satisfying feedback you receive when pressing the keys makes typing on them enjoyable. I wanted to take this experience even further by creating a custom layout. Many of the mainstream methods to custom mechanical keyboards are either limiting in their customization or very expensive so I thought I could take up this challenge to make a relatively inexpensive custom mechanical keyboard.

The first step to create this was to figure out the layout of the keys. I wanted something more compact that would take up less space on my desk, but still have all the functionality, if not more, of standard keyboards. I used a very handy tool called keyboard-layout-editor that made planning much easier. I decided to cut off the F1-F12 keys as they could easily be mapped to another layer, and add a column of macro keys to the left side that could be programmed to anything I might need. I then CADed the layout to create the plate for the switches as well as the case everything would sit in. I then 3D printed these, having to split them up into two separate parts because of the limitations of the bed size on the 3D printer. After a few prototypes, I was able to find a nice profile to hold the switches in nice and snug. Since I had a custom layout, a PCB with the layout doesn't exist and would be fairly pricy to have made by a company so the remaining option was to hand-wire everything together. With 70 switches and 3 solders per switch, I had to solder over 210 times, not including the wiring of the columns and rows to the microcontroller. After I finished soldering everything together and finishing up the board, I was very surprised that not one solder joint was faulty and the keyboard was working completely fine. Hey, I'm even typing this website on the keyboard right now. The macro keys on the side are some of the most useful keys I use and don't think I could ever go back to a normal keyboard. I highly recommend this project if you are interested in mechanical keyboards.

2.5D Pong

This is a simple pong game with a twist. It adds some aspects of the third dimension and physics to make the game more interesting than traditional pong. This was originally created for a final project in my engineering class but I decided to continue the project to where it is today. Future features might include the option for an online multi-player so users can play with others more easily. Feel free to try it out here: 2.5D Pong.
